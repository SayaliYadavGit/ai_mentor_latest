What are Cryptocurrencies, and How Do They Work?

Cryptocurrency is a digital or virtual form of currency that operates on decentralised networks based on blockchain technology and uses cryptography for security. The blockchain is a distributed ledger that records all transactions across a network of computers, ensuring transparency, security, and immutability.

Let‚Äôs break some of these terms down:

Digital currency: Cryptocurrency is like digital cash. It's not physical; it's just numbers on a computer.
Blockchain: A blockchain is a chain of information. When you send someone cryptocurrency, your transaction is added to a block. Once approved by the network, this block is added to a chain of other blocks, forming the "blockchain."
Distributed ledger: This is another way of saying that the blockchain is a digital ledger that records all cryptocurrency transactions. It's not stored in one place but is distributed across a network of computers, making it extremely secure and resistant to tampering.
Immutability: This is a fancy way of saying that once something is on the blockchain, it's there forever. Transactions on the blockchain are irreversible, providing a high level of security and trust. Once information is added to the blockchain, it's nearly impossible to change or delete.
Cryptography: Cryptography makes use of encrypted keys, or coded information, digital signatures, hash functions (algorithms) and other tools to secure cryptocurrency transactions. Your private key allows you to access and manage your crypto. Your public key is like your address, where people send crypto to you.

Cryptocurrency vs. Real Currency: What‚Äôs the Difference?

Here's where it gets interesting. Cryptocurrencies are fundamentally different from traditional fiat currencies in several ways:

Centralisation vs. decentralisation: Fiat currencies are issued and regulated by governments and central banks. Cryptocurrencies operate independently of any central authority. This decentralisation is a big deal because it means no one entity has control over your money.
Physical vs. digital: Fiat currencies exist in physical forms like paper bills and coins (although central bank digital currencies are gaining popularity). Cryptocurrencies are entirely digital and exist only in electronic form.
Global nature: Cryptocurrencies are borderless. You can send them to anyone, anywhere in the world, without the need for intermediaries like banks. This makes international transactions faster and cheaper.
Supply: Many cryptocurrencies, like Bitcoin, have a capped supply. For example, there will only ever be a certain number of Bitcoins in existence ‚Äì roughly 21 million (around 19 million of which have already been mined). The supply of fiat currencies, on the other hand, depends on the decisions made by central banks.
Privacy: Cryptocurrency transactions are pseudonymous. They're recorded on the blockchain, but your identity isn't tied to your wallet address unless you choose to reveal it. With fiat currencies, your transactions are often linked to your identity through banks and financial institutions.
Accessibility: Anyone with an internet connection can access and use cryptocurrencies, making them more inclusive than traditional banking systems that may not be available to everyone for several reasons.

There are multiple routes to crypto trading or investing, each with its own level of complexity and risk. Here are some of the different strategies to trade or invest in crypto:

Buy and Hold (Hodling): The simplest strategy involves purchasing cryptocurrencies and holding them in a wallet with the expectation that their value will increase over time. ‚ÄúHodlers‚Äù aims to benefit from long-term price appreciation.
Day trading: Day traders buy and sell cryptocurrencies within the same day to profit from short-term price fluctuations.
Swing trading: Swing traders hold positions for several days to weeks to capture price swings within a trend.
Futures trading: Futures contracts allow traders to speculate on the future price of cryptocurrencies without owning the underlying assets.
Options trading: Crypto options provide traders with the right (but not the obligation) to buy or sell cryptocurrencies at a predetermined price on or before a specified expiration date.
Algorithmic trading: Algorithmic or automated trading relies on pre-programmed trading strategies executed by computer algorithms. These algorithms can analyse crypto market data and execute trades at high speeds.
P2P trading: P2P platforms connect buyers and sellers directly, allowing them to trade cryptocurrencies without the need for an intermediary exchange. Users can negotiate prices and payment methods.
Yield farming: Yield farming involves providing liquidity to decentralized finance (DeFi) protocols in exchange for rewards. For example, staking is a common type of yield farming, where users lock up their coins in a blockchain network to support its operations and earn additional tokens or a share of transaction fees in return.
ICO: This method involves investing in newly launched cryptocurrencies or tokens during their Initial Coin Offering, or ICO.
Crypto CFDs: Contracts for Difference (CFDs) allow traders to speculate on the price movements of cryptocurrencies without owning the underlying assets. It offers leverage and the ability to profit from both rising and falling crypto markets.
